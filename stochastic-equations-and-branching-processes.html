Mixing Times for Markov Chains: Theory and Applications

FirstName LASTNAME Affiliation

The study of mixing times for Markov chains is fundamental in probability theory, with significant implications for statistics, theoretical computer science, and statistical physics. In this plenary talk, we will explore recent advances in the quantitative analysis of mixing times and their applications in diverse areas.

We provide a detailed discussion on various techniques used to bound the mixing time, including spectral gap methods, logarithmic Sobolev inequalities, and coupling arguments. Special emphasis will be given to the application of these techniques in the study of random walks on graphs, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods, and reversible vs. non-reversible chains.

Theorem Let \((X_t)_{t \geq 0}\) be a reversible Markov chain with transition matrix \(P\) and stationary distribution \(\pi\). Define the total variation distance to stationarity as: \[\tag{1} d(t) = \sup_{x} \| P^t(x,\cdot) - \pi \|_{TV}.\] Then, under appropriate spectral conditions, the mixing time \(t_{mix}(\varepsilon)\) satisfies the bound: \[\tag{2} t_{mix}(\varepsilon) \leq \frac{1}{1 - \lambda_2} \log \frac{1}{\varepsilon},\] where \(\lambda_2\) is the second-largest eigenvalue of \(P\).

Corollary For an ergodic, reversible Markov chain, if the spectral gap \(1 - \lambda_2\) is bounded below by a positive constant, then \(t_{mix}(\varepsilon)\) scales logarithmically in \(1/\varepsilon\), ensuring rapid convergence to stationarity.

These results have far-reaching applications in randomized algorithms, statistical sampling, and the analysis of large networks. We will conclude with a discussion of open problems and future directions in the study of mixing times.

Random Graphs: Structure, Evolution, and Applications

The study of random graphs has been a central topic in probability theory and combinatorics, with applications spanning network science, statistical physics, and theoretical computer science. In this plenary talk, we will explore fundamental results on the structural properties of random graphs and their dynamic evolution.

A key focus will be on Erdős–Rényi random graphs \(G(n, p)\) and their phase transitions, highlighting threshold phenomena for connectivity, giant component formation, and graph coloring. We will also examine more advanced models such as preferential attachment graphs, which capture scale-free network behavior, and random geometric graphs used in spatial networks.

Theorem For an Erdős–Rényi random graph \(G(n, p)\), the probability of the emergence of a giant component undergoes a sharp phase transition at \(p = \frac{1}{n}\). More precisely: \[\tag{1} \text{If } p < \frac{1}{n}, \text{ all components are of order } O(\log n).\] \[\tag{2} \text{If } p > \frac{1}{n}, \text{ a unique giant component of order } \Theta(n) \text{ appears}.\]

Corollary For sufficiently large \(n\), the expected size of the largest component satisfies: \[\tag{3} \mathbb{E}[C_1] \sim \begin{cases} (1 - \lambda) n, & p = \frac{c}{n}, c > 1,\\ O(\log n), & p = \frac{c}{n}, c < 1. \end{cases}\]

We will also discuss real-world applications of random graph models, including social networks, epidemiology, and information spread in large-scale systems.
